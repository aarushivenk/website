Overview

To determine what makes a good health and fitness app, I wanted to take a 3-pronged approach: compile app store feedback, user research, and use Figma to do a little bit of redesigning.

Background

There are more than 318,000 health and wellness apps currently in the app store, with 200+ being added each day. Especially due to COVID, more and more of us have turned to the virtual world to guide us on our health journeys. Home gym and workout equipment has had skyrocketing sales as people aim to improve or create healthy habits at home. One popular and easy way of keeping healthy is by using a habit tracker app.

With many on the market, I wanted to look at 3 pedometer apps-Pacer, StepsApp, Pedometer++-and determine what draws user attention and retains users.

A pedometer itself is a relatively simple concept, and on most iPhones, users already have a built-in health app that does just that. What I wanted to know was what additional features, interface, etc, gives an app the edge to attract users in.

User Research

Given my initial review of the app and comparing them to apple’s health app, I conducted 6 in-depth interviews with people of varying ages (19-55) and of varying exercise habits (low-high) for their opinions of each app.

General

  • Have you used health and fitness apps before?
  • How active would you say you are?
  • What is the biggest factor behind exercising for you?
  • Would you ever want to download a habit-tracker app?
  • What components would you expect to see in a step-counter app?

App-Specific

  • Are you able to find all of the expected components of a habit-tracker app?
  • What are your thoughts (positive and negative) about these apps?

Key-Takeaways

  • An attracting interface can greatly influence a user
  • Adding more features does not necessarily increase the attractiveness of an interface
  • Buttons should be intuitive and simple-you don’t want your users to struggle to find anything

Pacer

  • App Store Rating: 4.5/5
  • User Rating: 6.2/10
  • Lots of features
  • Informative stats
  • Complicated interface
  • Weak color palette
  • Intimidating

StepsApp

  • App Store Rating: 4.8/5
  • User Rating: 7.5/10
  • Simple interface
  • Strong color palette
  • Hard-to-access features

Pedometer++

  • App Store Rating: 4.6/5
  • User Rating: 4.8/10
  • Simple Interface
  • Motivating
  • Weak color palette
  • Not enough features

Audience

Based on the users’ responses, I was able to identify 3 different users, 1 for each app. Pedometer++ attracted more of an older, less tech-savvy audience. StepsApp seemed more towards those who wanted to track a little bit more than their steps and could use a little extra motivation. Pacer was for those who were more dedicated to fitness, specifically those who looked for the most out of their apps.

Redesign

I took to Figma to redesign the landing pages of the 3 apps based on my takeaways from the user research.

PACER

Overall, Pacer has many positive features and is widely-liked by its users. During my research, I found that in their initial impressions of the app, Pacer's biggest issue was a confusing user-flow.

STEPSAPP

PEDOMETER++

Pedometer++ was the easiest app to use in the three. While it's simpleness was a positive, some users felt it didn't have enough features to draw them towards it. One of its biggest strengths were in the awards, which are clever ways of motivating its users.

Reflection

I saw how apps were able to define their target audience by the features they were including, understanding what worked and what didn’t work.

I learned about the importance of colors and sizing, especially about their influence on a user’s thought process.

I experienced the importance of a landing page-they can determine how someone feels about the entire app.
